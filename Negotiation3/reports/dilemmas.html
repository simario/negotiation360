Two questions you answered deal with the interpersonal dimension of negotiation: asserting yourself and understanding others.

Research shows that many people feel that being relatively good at one of these comes at the cost of being less effective at the other. (Being firm about your demands could stress a relationship, for example; or appearing cooperative might be misread as weakness.) Nevertheless, some people feel equally good at both skills.

Two other questions address outcomes, specifically the ability to create value and capture your share of it.

In practice there is often a tension between these two skills. Value-creating trades require understanding the parties' complementary interests. But if you disclose your interests unilaterally, you might be exploited by others who bluff and conceal their true needs. Value will be created, but they may get most of it.

Your negotiation results are linked to how you relate to other parties. Think of the interpersonal scale as representing the means of negotiation and the outcome scale as the ends.

Putting the two scales together reveals common bargaining styles. The lines in the diagram intersect at the 0-0. The example here is for a user who has a rating of 4 for the four skills (counting out from the center for each one).

That balance may look nice, but some situations demand certain skills more than others.
