We used statistical tools to analyze responses from other negotiators who have taken this test. In order to make group comparisons, we "normalized" the data. That is, we compared each person's response to the average that we received in our pre-launch survey.


To see what we discovered, swipe to the next page.

We found five basic types, each one with a different balance of relative strengths. Some people were closely identified with a particular style, while others were near the boundary between one group and another. In your own dealings, you've likely encountered each one of these types.

To see the five bargaining styles, swipe through to the pages that follow.

Empathetic Value-Creators

30 percent of respondents

These people have above average confidence about understanding the true needs of their counterparts. They also believe they are good at spotting problem-solving opportunities.

On the other hand, they have less confidence about asserting their own interests and maximizing their share of the resulting value.

Assertive Value-Claimers

10 percent of respondents

These people are confident about their ability to advocate their own interests. As a result, they believe that they are able to capture a larger slice of the pie (though there may be less total value to win).

This profile is the mirror image of the Empathetic Value-Creators, as confidence about self-advocacy is offset by less assurance about relating to others.

Assertive Value-Creators

15 percent of respondents

Like Assertive Value-Claimers, these people are just as confident about their ability to advocate for themselves, but they are less certain about the value they obtain.

In reality some of them may perform very well, but as psychological "maximizers," no matter how much they get, they tend worry that they should have claimed an even a bigger share of the pie.

Relational Negotiators

25 percent of respondents

These people rated themselves strongest at self-advocacy and empathy. Maybe that self-assessment is accurate, though it raises the question of why, given superior relational skills, they don't do better at either creating or claiming value.

In reality some of them may perform very well, but as psychological "maximizers," no matter how much they get, they tend worry that they should have claimed an even a bigger share of the pie.

Outcome-Focused Negotiators

20 percent of respondents

These people rated themselves strongest on both ends of the outcome scale. They may be correct, though it raises a question of how it's possible to attain good results in spite of relational skills that are not as strong.
